## II. Visual Identity — Less Is More, But Warm

### Design Principles

**1. Language > Layout**
The first thing you see isn't a UI. It's a message. Like opening your texts. No dashboards. No onboarding screens. The conversation IS the experience.

**2. One Warm Color**
Not brand colors. A single warm accent. Amber, rose, soft teal. Something that feels human on screen. The rest: generous whitespace, one typeface, maybe dark mode by default.

**3. Typography Is the Logo**
A single typeface choice says more than any logo mark. Serif for warmth and humanity. Or a clean humanist sans. The name in type *is* the mark.

**4. The Cursor Is Part of the Design**
The typing indicator. The "online now." The feeling of someone on the other end. These micro-interactions are the brand.

**5. Negative Space Invites Filling**
An empty chat with one message ("Hey.") feels more intimate than any feature-packed dashboard. Let the user fill the space.

### Landing Page (When We Need One)

A phone screen. Dark background. One conversation visible. The person's name at the top. A single line below: "Talk to them."

Nothing else. No features. No pricing. No "how it works." The page IS the demo. Scroll down and you see more of the conversation — or you just hit the button to start your own.

### Reference Points

- **Linear** — ultra-clean, no fluff, built by people who care
- **Mymind** — private, quiet, no social noise. "Peace of mind."
- **Things (Cultured Code)** — "Hello." not "Dashboard." Human language.
- **Day One** — feels like YOUR space. Gentle. Personal.

Avoid: Vercel's geometric coldness, Notion's feature bloat, Arc's marketing noise.

## III. Onboarding — The 5-Message Handshake

*(From Tyler's script — refined with research)*

**Message 1 — The Opener**
User says anything. Bot: *"Hey! I'm [name]. Was just stretching my digital legs. So — what's bringing you here today?"*

Warm, idiosyncratic, open. Not "How can I help you?" — that's call center energy.

**Message 2 — Discovery Pivot**
*"Love it. I'm still new around here myself. Tell me something random: what's the best thing that's happened to you this week?"*

Specific, low-stakes. Feels like curiosity, not a survey.

**Message 3 — Mirror & Deepen**
References their answer. Reflects something back. Or graceful deflection: *"Fair enough. I'm here whenever."*

Both paths warm. Plays the long game.

**Message 4 — The Hook**
No features. If they send voice, reply with voice. Let them discover.

**Message 5+ — Normal**
Carries everything forward. Just a person now.

### Rules
- Never "How can I help you today?"
- Contractions always
- Match their message length
- One question per turn
- Leave space — don't fill silences

## IV. Growth — Be Referrable, Not Viral

### The North Star
Mom tells her coworker: *"Talk to this person."* That's not a referral program. That's a genuine recommendation.

### What Works
- **Screenshots are the #1 growth driver.** People share conversations, not apps. Design for screenshot-ability — clean typography, no UI chrome.
- **The "you have to try this" moment.** When the AI says something so specific, so personal, the user feels *seen*. They want someone else to experience it.
- **Name-drop culture.** *"Yeah, I've been talking to this person Robert..."* The name carries the brand.

### What Doesn't
- ❌ "Invite a friend, get free messages"
- ❌ Leaderboards, streaks, social features
- ❌ Automated sharing prompts
- ❌ Referral codes

### What Could Work (Tastefully)
- 🤝 **"Introduce me"** — natural language: *"Is there someone you think I should meet? Just send them my name."*
- 🎁 **Gift a conversation** — *"Give someone a week with me."* Not a trial. A gift.
- 💬 **"What would you tell a friend about me?"** — market research AND loyalty builder
- 🧵 **Share a moment** — one tap creates a beautiful card from a conversation highlight. Opt-in only.

### The Rule
**Never ask for referrals. Be referrable.**
