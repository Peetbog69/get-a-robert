## 1. Brand Case Studies

### 1.1 Apple — The Gold Standard

Apple's design philosophy can be captured in one phrase: **"Less is better."** From hardware to website, Apple treats minimalism as a signal of luxury and sophistication.

**What makes it work visually:**

- **Brutal reduction.** Every element must justify its existence. If it doesn't serve the message, it's removed.
- **Typography as hero.** Apple uses its custom typeface **SF Pro** (San Francisco), a neo-grotesque sans-serif inspired by Helvetica and FF DIN. On the web, headings are massive and confident — often 64px+ — with generous line-height.
- **Product photography is everything.** Apple's product shots are shot on stark white or subtly gradient backgrounds. The product is the only star. No competing elements.
- **Content hierarchy through size, not decoration.** Apple rarely uses borders, boxes, or backgrounds to separate sections. Instead, it relies on massive size contrast, whitespace, and a single scrolling rhythm.
- **Color as accent only.** The website is overwhelmingly monochrome — white, near-black text, and the occasional product color or subtle blue link.
- **Scroll-driven storytelling.** Each scroll position reveals exactly one narrative beat. Apple pioneered the "scroll-jacking" product page where each scroll tick advances a 3D product animation.

**Key takeaway:** Apple's minimalism is *editorial*. It treats a product page like a magazine spread — photography-dominant, typography-driven, and ruthlessly edited.

### 1.2 Linear — The SaaS Design Paradigm

Linear.app is so influential that an entire design trend — **"linear design"** — was named after it. It's the canonical example of how SaaS products approach minimalism in 2025–2026.

**What makes it work visually:**

- **Dark mode as default.** Linear's interface is a dark, near-black canvas (`#0D0E12` / Woodsmoke tones). This creates an immediate "developer tool" feel and makes accent colors pop dramatically.
- **Monochrome with one accent.** Linear's primary brand color is a **subtle desaturated indigo-blue**. Nearly everything else is grayscale. When color appears, it means something — status, priority, action.
- **Bold typography, minimal words.** Headlines are set in massive weights, often 600–700 weight Inter (or similar). Copy is stripped to the bone.
- **High contrast, no clutter.** Every UI element has clear purpose. The interface uses subtle borders (1px, low opacity) rather than background colors to delineate sections.
- **"Linear" reading direction.** Content flows top-to-bottom, left-to-right in logical progression. Users scan naturally. This reduces cognitive load — a core principle of "linear design" as defined by the trend.
- **Subtle glassmorphism.** Linear uses backdrop blur and translucent surfaces sparingly for depth — never as decoration.
- **Micro-interactions as feedback.** Every click, hover, and state change has a subtle animated response.

**2025 evolution:** Linear has progressively stripped back even their blue accent, moving toward a more monochrome black/white palette with even fewer colors. This mirrors the broader trend of "warm minimalism" — less is more, but it must feel warm, not cold.

**Key takeaway:** Linear proves that a SaaS product can use "boring" as a feature. The interface fades into the background so the work can take center stage.

### 1.3 Nous Research — Open-Source AI, Brutalist Elegance

Nous Research's website and branding take a distinctly different approach from polished Silicon Valley minimalism. It's closer to **Swiss brutalist** — raw, typographic, and unapologetically direct.

**What makes it work visually:**

- **Helvetica everywhere.** Nous's brand booklet explicitly names Helvetica as their typeface — a neo-grotesque design influenced by Akzidenz-Grotesk and Swiss design traditions. This is a deliberate choice: Helvetica communicates *neutrality, clarity, and seriousness.*
- **Monochrome, high-contrast.** The site uses stark black-on-white (or white-on-black) with almost no color. When color appears, it's minimal and purposeful.
- **No decoration.** The site is essentially pure information architecture. Navigation, content, links. No gradients, no shadows, no "design." This signals open-source authenticity — "we're a research lab, not a marketing company."
- **Typography as interface.** Headings and body text carry the entire visual weight. Sizing, spacing, and weight create all hierarchy.
- **Asymmetric layouts.** Swiss design principles shine through in the occasional off-grid placement and bold use of negative space.

**Key takeaway:** Nous Research's design says "we are serious researchers, not a polished consumer product." The undesign is the design.

### 1.4 Figure31 — An Artist's Digital Canvas

Figure31 is the portfolio of Loucas Braconnier, a digital artist and creative coder. It represents the *artistic* end of minimalism — a single-page, scrolling narrative that blurs the line between portfolio, artwork, and blockchain experiment.

**What makes it work visually:**

- **Extreme reduction.** The site is essentially one long scroll with minimal navigation. Content is the interface.
- **Experimental typography.** Large, sometimes unconventional type treatments that treat text as image.
- **Black and white with occasional digital color.** The palette is predominantly monochrome with moments of screen-like color — fitting for a digital artist working with blockchain and on-chain art.
- **Raw HTML aesthetic.** There's an intentional simplicity to the markup — it feels hand-crafted, not templated.
- **No UI chrome.** The work speaks. The "website" is almost invisible.

**Key takeaway:** When you're showcasing creative work, the frame should be invisible. Figure31's site is a gallery wall, not a designed page.

## 2. Key Principles of Minimalist Digital Design (2025–2026)

### 2.1 Whitespace as Active Element

In 2026, whitespace is no longer "empty space" — it's the primary design material. Key patterns:

- **Generous padding.** Sections use 120px+ vertical padding. Content areas breathe with 80–120px horizontal margins on desktop.
- **Negative space creates hierarchy.** Rather than rules, boxes, or backgrounds, modern minimalism uses space itself to group and separate content.
- **"Room to breathe."** As Linear's brand guidelines state: "Make them big or make them small, but give them room to breathe. They shouldn't feel cramped or cluttered."

### 2.2 Typography as Interface

Typography has become the primary UI element:

- **Massive display type.** Headlines at 64–120px, often in variable fonts that scale fluidly.
- **Weight, not color, for hierarchy.** Bold (700+), medium (500), and regular (400) create all the visual hierarchy needed.
- **Single typeface families.** Many brands use one type family with multiple weights rather than pairing different fonts. Apple (SF Pro), OpenAI (OpenAI Sans), Linear (Inter).
- **Minimal text.** Every word is edited. Paragraphs are short. CTAs are 2–4 words maximum.

### 2.3 Restrained Color

The dominant approach:

- **Neutral base.** White, off-white (`#FAFAFA`, `#F5F5F5`), near-black (`#111111`, `#0D0E12`), or true dark (`#000000`).
- **One accent color.** A single saturated color used exclusively for interactive elements, CTAs, and key brand moments. Everything else is grayscale.
- **Color = meaning.** When color appears, it carries semantic weight — a status indicator, a link, a primary action.

### 2.4 Micro-interactions as "Aliveness"

Minimalist interfaces can feel cold without motion. The 2026 approach:

- **Every state has a transition.** Buttons, toggles, inputs — all have 150–300ms smooth transitions between states.
- **Feedback is immediate.** Hover states, click states, loading states — nothing is static.
- **Motion provides affordance.** A card that expands, a menu that slides — motion tells the user what happened and what's possible.
- **Subtle, not spectacular.** Micro-interactions in 2026 are dampened — ease-out curves, small displacements, gentle opacity changes. They feel like *breathing*, not *performing*.

### 2.5 Dark Mode as Default (Not an Option)

2026 has solidified dark mode as the standard for tech products:

- **SaaS products lead.** Linear, Raycast, Vercel, and most developer tools default to dark.
- **Not pure black.** Near-blacks (`#0D0E12`, `#111111`, `#1A1A1A`) reduce eye strain and allow for depth through subtle layering.
- **Light mode is still required.** Accessibility and user preference means systems must support both. But the *brand identity* is now defined in dark mode first.

### 2.6 Design Tokens & Systematic Thinking

Design tokens — named, reusable design variables — are the backbone of 2026 design systems:

- **Colors, spacing, typography** are defined as tokens, not hardcoded values
- Enables consistent scaling across products and platforms
- Makes dark/light mode switching trivial
- Critical for AI-assisted design workflows

## 3. Color Philosophy in Modern Tech Branding

### 3.1 The Monochrome + Accent Pattern

The most common color strategy for tech brands in 2026:

| Role | Typical Approach | Examples |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| Background | Near-black or off-white | `#0D0E12` (Linear), `#141413` (Anthropic dark), `#FAF9F5` (Anthropic light), `#000000` (OpenAI) |
| Text primary | White on dark, near-black on light | High contrast ratios (WCAG AAA preferred) |
| Text secondary | 60–70% opacity of primary | `rgba(255,255,255,0.6)` on dark |
| Accent | One saturated color | `#10A37F` (OpenAI/ChatGPT green), Linear's desaturated indigo, `#D97757` (Anthropic orange) |
| Borders/Dividers | Very low opacity | 1px at 8–12% opacity |

### 3.2 Specific Brand Color Palettes

**OpenAI (2025 Rebrand):**
- Primary dark: `#000000`
- Primary light: `#FFFFFF`
- Accent (ChatGPT): `#10A37F` (green)
- Palette: Grays and blues evoking "openness and possibility"
- Designed to feel "minimal, authoritative, and forward-looking"

**Anthropic / Claude (by Geist):**
- Dark: `#141413`
- Light: `#FAF9F5`
- Mid gray: (neutral body)
- Accent orange: `#D97757`
- Accent blue: `#6A9BCC`
- Accent green: `#788C5D`
- A warm, approachable palette designed to stand apart from "tech tropes"

**Linear:**
- Brand color: Subtle desaturated indigo-blue
- Background: Woodsmoke (`#0D0E12` approximately)
- Neutral: Oslo Gray, Black Haze, White
- Comfortable against both light and dark backgrounds

**DeepSeek:**
- Bright blue whale logo on white
- Rounded, friendly wordmark
- Warm and approachable — deliberately counter to "sleek and impersonal" AI branding

### 3.3 Dark Mode Defaults

The 2026 status quo:

- **Tech products launch with dark mode as primary experience**
- **Monochrome dark palettes use 3–5 gray stops** between background and surface, creating depth through layering
- **Accent colors are brighter in dark mode** — a color that works at 100% saturation on dark may need to be darker for light mode
- **Not pure white text** — slightly warm whites (`#FAFAFA`, `#F5F5F5`) reduce eye strain

### 3.4 Color Psychology

- **Blue** dominates 61% of top tech company logos — signals trust, stability, intelligence
- **Black/white/monochrome** signals sophistication, confidence, and "premium"
- **Single accent** signals focus, clarity, and brand restraint
- **Warm neutrals** (Anthropic's `#FAF9F5`, orange accents) signal approachability and humanity — a reaction *against* cold, sterile tech
- **Green accents** (OpenAI `#10A37F`) signal growth, safety, and "for humanity"

## 4. The Psychology of Minimalism

### 4.1 Why "Less" Signals Confidence

Minimalism in digital products works psychologically on multiple levels:

**Cognitive Science:**

- **Choice overload (Hick's Law).** More options = more cognitive load = more anxiety. Minimalist UIs reduce decisions per screen, making products feel *easier* — even if they're powerful.
- **Visual processing speed.** Users form aesthetic judgments in <50ms. Clean designs process faster — they feel "right" before the user knows why.
- **Reduced extraneous cognitive load.** When decoration is stripped away, users focus on content and task. The UI becomes transparent.

**Behavioral Economics:**

- **The paradox of choice.** Barry Schwartz's research shows that fewer options increase satisfaction. Minimalist design applies this to visual elements — fewer competing elements = more satisfying experience.
- **Signaling theory.** Minimalism signals *confidence*. A company that doesn't need to shout, decorate, or over-explain is a company that believes in its product. Apple doesn't use exclamation marks. Linear doesn't use gradients to "wow" you.

**Emotional Response:**

- **Calm.** Clean interfaces reduce cortisol. There's nothing to "figure out."
- **Trust.** Minimalism communicates transparency. Nothing is hidden behind decoration.
- **Premium perception.** Luxury brands — from Apple to Rimowa to Aesop — use minimalism to signal quality. The product doesn't need dressing up.

### 4.2 The Counter-Reaction: "Warm Minimalism"

2026 has seen a correction to *cold* minimalism:

- **"Emotional minimalism"** — less expression feels more professional, but too little feels inhuman
- **Human touches.** Hand-drawn elements, imperfect textures, warm neutrals, organic shapes — these soften rigid minimalism
- **The Anthropic approach.** Geist's work for Anthropic deliberately chose a quirky type palette (Styrene + Tiempos) and warm color system to avoid "tech tropes"
- **Anti-AI-sameness.** As AI generates increasingly polished designs, human imperfections become a differentiator. Grain, hand-drawn elements, organic shapes.

## 5. How AI Companies Approach Design

### 5.1 OpenAI — The 2025 Rebrand

OpenAI's first-ever rebrand (February 2025) is the definitive case study in how the leading AI company approaches design.

**What changed:**

- **Custom typeface: OpenAI Sans.** Designed by ABC Dinamo, a bespoke typeface blending "geometric precision with a softer, more rounded character." This is a perfect microcosm of the brand — technically sophisticated yet human-feeling.
- **Refined "Blossom" logo.** Lines thickened and made uniform width.
- **New "point" mark.** A simple dot representing the starting point from which AI vectors outward.
- **Grays and blues palette.** Evoking "openness and possibility."
- **Motion design by Studio Dumbar.** "Organic motion design" with "restraint and meaningful, poetic gestures" — a state of "academic beauty."

**Design philosophy:**

> "More organic and more human, without losing the sense of authority, technological sophistication and research-focused underpinnings."

**Key takeaway:** OpenAI's design is *restrained authority*. It says "we are serious researchers" while also saying "this technology is for everyone."

### 5.2 Anthropic / Claude — Design That Stands Apart

Anthropic worked with agency Geist for 2.5 years to build a brand "infused with their values, that stands apart in a category rife with tech tropes."

**Design decisions:**

- **Pure typographic logo** with a single standout detail (the slash — a reference to code and the future)
- **Type pairing: Styrene (Commercial Type) + Tiempos (Klim).** "Technically refined and charmingly quirky" — deliberately *not* another Inter/Helvetica clone.
- **Warm color system.** Orange (`#D97757`), blue (`#6A9BCC`), green (`#788C5D`) on warm off-white (`#FAF9F5`) and near-black (`#141413`). Warm enough for marketing, functional enough for product UI.
- **Illustration language** that "doubles down on human values"
- **UI component system** that "puts function first without losing the soul of the brand"
- **Brand typography for artifacts:** Poppins (headings) + Lora (body text)

**Philosophy:** Anthropic's brand embodies their mission — safety, human values, technical excellence. The warmth says "we care about humanity." The precision says "we know what we're doing."

### 5.3 DeepSeek — Warmth Over Sterility

DeepSeek takes a notably different approach from Western AI companies:

- **Logo: A plump, bright blue whale** that appears to jump across the screen
- **Rounded, friendly wordmark** — approachable, not intimidating
- **Deliberately warm.** In an industry "often characterised by sleek and impersonal branding," DeepSeek chose warmth
- **Emotional connection** as primary design goal — the visual identity aims to achieve "a touchpoint emotionally with its users"
- **The whale symbolizes** depth, intelligence, and approachability

**Key insight:** DeepSeek's branding is a strategic counter-position to Western AI minimalism. Where OpenAI and Anthropic use restraint to signal authority, DeepSeek uses warmth to signal accessibility. Both are valid.

### 5.4 Character.AI — Playful, Not Corporate

Character.AI takes an even further departure — where other AI companies signal *intelligence*, Character.AI signals *fun*:

- Bright, playful interface with chat bubbles and character avatars
- Designed for a consumer audience, not enterprise
- The product IS the brand — no separate corporate identity layer
- "Characters" as the organizing principle

### 5.5 The Pattern: Three AI Branding Archetypes

| Archetype | Visual Language | Examples | Strategy |
|-----------|----------------|----------|----------|
| **Research Authority** | Monochrome, typographic, restrained | OpenAI, Anthropic, Nous Research | "Trust us — we're serious" |
| **Warm Accessible** | Rounded, colorful, friendly | DeepSeek | "AI is for everyone" |
| **Playful Consumer** | Bright, character-driven, gamified | Character.AI | "AI is fun" |

## 6. Motion Design Trends

### 6.1 The Philosophy Shift: "Thought, Not Animation"

The defining shift in 2026 motion design is philosophical. Motion isn't *decoration* — it's *communication*. As Studio Dumbar describes their OpenAI work:

> "We applied organic motion design with restraint and meaningful, poetic gestures — enhancing a state of academic beauty."

**Key principles:**

- Motion should explain *what happened*
- Motion should suggest *what's possible*
- Motion should feel like the interface is *thinking*, not *performing*

### 6.2 Page Transitions

**Current best practices:**

- **Cross-fades over cuts.** Opacity transitions (200–400ms) between pages feel smoother than instant changes
- **Shared element transitions.** Elements that persist between pages (navigation, headers) animate into new positions rather than disappearing/reappearing
- **Directional motion.** Forward navigation moves left-to-right (in LTR languages); back navigation reverses. This builds spatial mental models.
- **Scroll-driven reveals.** Elements fade/slide in as they enter the viewport — but subtly. Opacity 0→1 over 400ms, with slight Y offset (20px → 0)

### 6.3 Scroll Animations

**2026 trends:**

- **Parallax is dead; depth-of-field is in.** Rather than elements moving at different speeds, modern scroll effects use blur and opacity to create depth (elements in "background" are slightly blurred, come into focus as user scrolls)
- **Sticky positioning as storytelling.** Key product visuals pin in place while text scrolls over them — Apple pioneered this, it's now universal
- **Scroll-triggered video.** Short video clips play as the user scrolls past, creating a sense of watching rather than reading
- **Organic motion.** Gentle transforms on gradient layers and curved shapes to create "natural rhythm" (per Elementor's 2026 trend report)

### 6.4 Hover States & Micro-interactions

**The "thinking" aesthetic:**

- **150–250ms transitions** — fast enough to feel responsive, slow enough to feel considered
- **Ease-out curves** — elements decelerate into position, never snap
- **Scale micro-adjustments.** Buttons scale to 97% on press, 102% on hover — subtle enough to feel organic
- **Color shifts, not additions.** A button doesn't get a border on hover — it shifts saturation or lightness by 5–10%
- **State changes through opacity.** Secondary text fades from 60% to 80% opacity on hover rather than changing color
- **Loading states tell a story.** Instead of spinners, modern UIs use skeleton screens, progress indicators, and optimistic UI updates

### 6.5 The Lottie/Rive Revolution

- **Lottie animations** — lightweight, scalable, code-driven — have become the standard for complex micro-interactions
- **Rive** (state-machine-driven animations) is gaining ground for interactive motion
- **CSS-only** for simple transitions; Lottie/Rive for complex ones
- **Performance-first.** Motion that causes layout shifts or jank is worse than no motion

## 7. Typography Trends 2025–2026

### 7.1 The "Quiet Sans-Serif" Era

In 2026, tech brands are moving away from the "generic sans-serif" look and toward typefaces with *personality* — but still restrained.

**The dominant approach:** One type family, used well.

### 7.2 What Top Brands Are Actually Using

| Brand | Typeface(s) | Style | Notes |
|-------|------------|-------|-------|
| **Apple** | SF Pro (San Francisco) | Neo-grotesque sans-serif | Custom, inspired by Helvetica + FF DIN. Variable font with optical sizes. |
| **OpenAI** | OpenAI Sans | Geometric humanist sans-serif | Custom by ABC Dinamo (2025). "Geometric precision with softer character." |
| **Anthropic (brand)** | Styrene + Tiempos | Technical sans + quirky serif | By Commercial Type and Klim. "Technically refined and charmingly quirky." |
| **Anthropic (artifacts)** | Poppins + Lora | Geometric sans + readable serif | Headings: Poppins (24pt+). Body: Lora. Fallbacks: Arial / Georgia. |
| **Linear** | Inter (inferred) | Modern neo-grotesque | Clean, highly legible, wide range of weights. |
| **Nous Research** | Helvetica | Classic neo-grotesque | Deliberately traditional Swiss choice. |
| **DeepSeek** | Rounded sans-serif | Friendly, approachable | Matches whale logo warmth. |

### 7.3 The Big Typography Trends

**1. Variable Fonts Are Standard**

Variable fonts allow a single font file to contain multiple weights, widths, and optical sizes. Key benefits:

- **Performance.** One file instead of 6–9
- **Fluid scaling.** Typography can scale with viewport width seamlessly
- **Optical sizing.** Text looks correct at every size without manual adjustment

**2. Display → Body Separation**

Many brands now use two typefaces:

- **Display/Heading:** A characterful, sometimes idiosyncratic typeface used at large sizes (>24px)
- **Body/UI:** A highly readable workhorse for small sizes and long text

Anthropic exemplifies this with Styrene (display) + Tiempos (body), or Poppins (headings) + Lora (body text).

**3. "Expressive Minimalism"**

The 2026 typography zeitgeist is captured by the phrase "expressive minimalism":

- Moving away from "safe, neutral sans-serifs (goodbye, generic tech vibes)" — ArtCoast Design
- Toward "fonts that have a distinct voice"
- Bold futurism meets elegant restraint
- Geometric, massive, and impossible to ignore

**4. Rounded Softness**

A reaction to the angular precision of 2010s minimalism:

- Rounded typefaces and softened corners
- "Keep your current type and simply round edges or choose a softer cut" — VistaPrint 2026 trends
- Signals warmth, approachability, and humanity

**5. Type as Graphic**

Typography filling the role that images once did:

- Massive display text that bleeds off screen
- Text used as texture, pattern, or background
- Words treated as visual shapes, not just readable content

**6. Anti-AI Texture**

As AI-generated design becomes ubiquitous, "human" typography differentiates:

- Grain, sketchy lines, imperfect letterforms
- Collage, messy typography, real-world texture
- "Using AI for ideas and raw visuals, then breaking them on purpose" — Fontfabric 2026

### 7.4 Fonts That Signal Different Things

| Signal | Typeface Characteristics | Examples |
|--------|-------------------------|----------|
| **Sophistication** | Neo-grotesque, precise, neutral | Helvetica, SF Pro, Inter |
| **Authority** | Geometric, bold, uppercase | OpenAI Sans, custom display faces |
| **Approachability** | Rounded, humanist, warm | DeepSeek's rounded sans, Poppins |
| **Technical excellence** | Monospace or technical sans | Styrene, JetBrains Mono |
| **Creativity** | Serif pairings, quirky details | Tiempos, Lora, editorial serifs |
| **Luxury** | High contrast, thin weights, wide tracking | Custom elegant serifs |

## 8. Practical Takeaways

### 8.1 For a Homepage

**Structure:**

1. **Hero: One sentence, massive type, no decoration.** The product name or value prop at 64–120px, dark background, one CTA button (your accent color). Nothing else.
2. **Product demonstration.** Show, don't tell. A screenshot, video, or interactive demo that proves the value in <3 seconds.
3. **Key features: 3–4 cards, minimal text.** Each card has an icon/illustration, a bold heading, and one sentence. Cards are monochrome — accent only on hover.
4. **Social proof.** Logos of customers/users, but in grayscale, small, and unobtrusive.
5. **CTA repetition.** Same button as hero, with one sentence of supporting text.
6. **Footer: Links only.** No decoration, no background color change. Just organized text links.

**Color system:**

- Background: Near-black (`#0D0E12` or similar) or off-white (`#FAFAFA`)
- Text: White (on dark) or near-black (on light)
- Accent: One color, used only for CTAs and key interactive elements
- No decorative colors anywhere

**Typography system:**

- One heading typeface, one body typeface (or one for both)
- At least 4–5 weight grades (300, 400, 500, 600, 700)
- Massive size contrast: hero at 80px, body at 16–18px
- Generous line-height: 1.1–1.2 for headings, 1.5–1.6 for body

**Motion:**

- Elements fade in on scroll (opacity 0→1, translateY 20px→0, 400ms)
- Button hover: subtle scale (1.02) and background brightness shift
- Page transitions: cross-fade, 300ms
- No autoplay video with sound
- No parallax, no gratuitous animation

### 8.2 For a Brand Identity

**Logo:**

- Typographic or minimal mark
- Works in monochrome
- Recognizable at 16px (favicon) and 200px (hero)
- One clever detail — OpenAI's "point," Anthropic's "slash," Linear's logomark

**Color palette:**

- One dark, one light, one accent, 2–3 neutral grays
- Define in design tokens from day one
- Dark mode and light mode from the start

**Typography:**

- Choose typefaces that you can *own* over time
- If budget allows, commission or license a custom typeface (OpenAI's approach with ABC Dinamo)
- If not, choose a distinctive but restrained pairing — never default system fonts for brand
- Define heading/body pairing and stick to it ruthlessly

**Photography/Illustration:**

- If photography: editorial, high-contrast, consistent color grading
- If illustration: one distinctive style, used sparingly, never as decoration
- Anthropic's approach: illustration language that "doubles down on human values"

### 8.3 For Product UI

**Layout:**

- Single-column wherever possible
- Generous whitespace — 24px minimum between elements, 48–80px between sections
- Fixed sidebars only when absolutely necessary
- Content flows top-to-bottom in reading order

**Components:**

- Buttons: one primary style (accent color), one secondary (ghost/outline), one tertiary (text-only). That's it.
- Inputs: border-bottom or full-border at 1px. No backgrounds unless focused.
- Cards: subtle borders (1px at 8% opacity), no shadows unless elevated (modals)
- Navigation: minimal. 3–5 items maximum. Hamburger on mobile only if items exceed 3.

**States:**

- Every interactive element has: default, hover, focus, active, disabled
- Transitions between states: 150–250ms ease-out
- Loading: skeleton screens > spinners
- Empty states: helpful, not decorative

**Dark mode:**

- Define as tokens: `--bg-primary`, `--bg-secondary`, `--text-primary`, etc.
- Not pure black background: `#0D0E12`, `#111111`, or tinted dark (e.g., `#141413` with blue warmth)
- Text: off-white (`#FAFAFA` or `rgba(255,255,255,0.87)`), never pure white at full opacity
- Accent colors may need dark mode variants (brighter for dark backgrounds)

### 8.4 The Checklist: Does It Belong?

Before adding any element, ask:

1. **What job does this do?** If it's purely decorative, remove it.
2. **Could whitespace do this instead?** Often, space communicates separation better than a line or box.
3. **Does this reduce cognitive load?** If an element adds choices, questions, or visual noise, it's hurting UX.
4. **Would Apple do this?** (Or Linear, or whoever your design north star is.)
5. **Is this accessible?** Does it meet WCAG AA contrast ratios? Does it work with keyboard navigation? Screen readers?

### 8.5 Recommended Starting Stack

For a new digital brand in 2026:

| Category | Recommendation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Primary typeface** | Inter (free, versatile, modern) or SF Pro (if Apple ecosystem) |
| **Display typeface** | A distinctive alternative — Instrument Sans, Satoshi, or a custom choice |
| **Body typeface** | Inter, Lora (if serif body), or your primary if it reads well at 14–16px |
| **Color token system** | Define in Figma variables / CSS custom properties before any screen design |
| **Design tool** | Figma with variables and design tokens |
| **Motion** | CSS transitions for simple states, Framer Motion / GSAP for complex scroll animations |
| **Icons** | One consistent set — Phosphor, Lucide, or custom. No mixing. |
| **Design system** | Start with tokens (colors, spacing, typography), then components |
