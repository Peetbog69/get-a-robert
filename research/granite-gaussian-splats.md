II. What is Gaussian Splatting?

3D Gaussian Splatting or GSP refers to a modern digital rendering technique used in computer graphics for transforming photographs or videos of real-world objects into high-resolution 3D models. It involves breaking down the visual data captured from a scene or object into mathematically described 'gaussian splats,' which are basically a collection of weighted Gaussians (probabilistic distributions). These gaussian splats allow for an accurate representation and reconstruction of complex three-dimensional shapes.

III. Gaussian Splatting vs Meshes

Traditional methods like polygonal mesh technology involve modeling objects by dividing their surfaces into triangles or other simple polygons connected at vertices. While this method works well with smooth, regular structures, it struggles with detailed, irregular shapes that are common in real-world objects. In contrast, GSP represents an advancement over meshes as it excels at capturing the complexity and detail of real-world objects.

IV. Animation Possibilities

One notable advantage of Gaussian Splatting lies within its animation capabilities. Unlike mesh models which require painstakingly animating each vertex or segment individually, 3D Gaussian splatting offers a more efficient approach. Transform manipulations - rotation, scaling, shearing – are relatively straightforward as they involve adjusting the parameters associated with respective gaussian splats. 

Moreover, an innovative concept known as '4D Gaussian Splatting' takes this process even further by incorporating time (the fourth dimension) into the model. This opens up avenues for creating animations directly from static 3D scans without needing additional keyframe animation work.
